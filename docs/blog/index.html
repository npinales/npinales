Social Media in Psychological Sciences

Given the ever-transforming landscape of social media, it is difficult for those of us engaged in scientific psychological research to understand how best to use social media to connect with other professionals, disseminate our research, and learn about our peers’ research.

From Digital Marketer to Researcher

Prior to pursuing an education and career in Clinical Psychology and Clinical Research, I was a digital marketer on the pulse of emerging platforms, platform conventions, and digital content strategy day in and day out. During this time, I developed brand strategy, served on a crisis communications team, wrote press releases, managed social media ads, and was even the lead script writer, script editor, and on-camera personality for the YouTube channel of an e-commerce company—becoming, funnily enough, somewhat of a micro-celebrity in the Latvian e-commerce sphere.

I left this field four years ago, and in that time, many of the commonplace platforms and approaches to social media have become irrelevant.

Using X and LinkedIn

Take X, for example. What once hosted a thriving community of engaged PhDs and PhD students chatting about the rewards and struggles of the field has quickly become a ghost town, where tumbleweed now weaves through sparse posts with links to recent manuscripts. Even LinkedIn, which is my current professional social media platform of choice, can be rife with sales pitches and AI slop. That said, LinkedIn can provide a great opportunity to stay abreast of unique expert insights, relevant events to attend, and the accomplishments of peers.

One of my favorite recent LinkedIn follows is Dr. Rachel Wood, an expert on the psychology of AI, who shares her professional perspectives on how AI is changing the way clients engage with AI to supplement—or replace—therapy, the dangers and benefits this poses, and how psychologists should approach client AI use in their sessions. She posts frequently, replies to comments on her posts, and interacts with other people’s posts, all in intentional ways.

Dr. Wood’s approach differs vastly from the way many social-media-averse professionals use social media, which is often to advertise their own work and achievements, share a conference photo or link to a publication, and then exit the platform until there is a new accomplishment to share. Her use of the platform demonstrates how social media should be used professionally: as a give-and-take, a way to build a meaningful network by sharing original thoughts and building relationships with those in her network.

Given how social media can overexpose us to the ills of the world, I very much understand and relate to the desire to be on social media as little as possible. But as researchers, we need to move toward considering social media as another important avenue, beyond conferences and journals, through which to engage with others about our areas of interest and expertise.

Public Dissemination of Research

At the 2025 Association for Cognitive and Behavioral Therapies Convention in New Orleans, I attended an informative panel on science advocacy, which featured valuable insights on advancing psychological science across media and a variety of work settings. Panel speakers like Dr. Jonathan Abramowitz and Dr. Samuel Spencer underlined the need for psychologists to use media and social media to share evidence-based information about the field with the general public and other professionals, meeting them where they are.

This public dissemination of information is especially important in a world where people get their information about psychology online, often through irreputable sources. I learned this firsthand during my undergraduate education, when I assisted with a study evaluating students’ ability to discern accurate from inaccurate information about OCD on TikTok. These students, who were all in Psychology 101, were not able to tell the difference.

In order to combat this misinformation crisis, psychologists need to be on the front lines delivering accurate, evidence-based insights about the field in accessible ways to mitigate the harm currently being caused by individuals and groups spreading false information about mental health—who range from being well-intentioned but misinformed to being unlicensed and profit-seeking.

The Need for Edutainment

Perhaps, as a field, we need figureheads similar to Dr. Mike on YouTube, who shares meaningful and accurate information about medicine with millions of subscribers in a way that captures their attention. I am sure many experts in their field never imagined their future would consist of YouTube videos with titles like ‘Doctor Reacts to My Strange Addiction’ or ‘Doctor Reacts to Deadliest Catch Injuries.’

But in a world where many people are exhausted from balancing the ever-increasing demands of work, school, and family life, the average person likely lacks the energy, desire, or ability to read serious, dense, scholarly content in their free time. This does not mean they are shut off to learning, only that they may prefer to learn in a way that blends education with entertainment.

While people may click on Dr. Mike’s videos because of a reference to Marvel, while watching the video they are also likely to learn valuable and relevant information about injury prevention and how to mitigate injury once it occurs.

How to Use Social Media for Science

In his book, How to Be a Modern Scientist, Jeff Leek shares a number of suggestions for how researchers can use social media, such as:

  • Follow relevant people and journals
  • Promote the work of other people by curating valuable content
  • Share work that you created
  • Make online friends and meet them offline
  • Add your commentary about other people’s work
  • Make YouTube Shorts, TikToks, or Instagram Reels if you are open to using video
  • Create infographics or images on Tumblr or Instagram
  • Use X and LinkedIn if you prefer to share text-based posts

The ever-growing and ever-changing landscape of social media requires us to stay vigilant and curious about how best to connect with research, researchers, and the broader public. That said, the digital world can provide great opportunities for us to do all of these things, and some of these opportunities are only possible with social media.

Let us put our fear and reservations aside and get started, one post at a time.
